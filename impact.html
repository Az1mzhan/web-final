Impact of Fast Food on Health

Welcome to the dedicated "Impact of Fast Food" page at FFH. In this comprehensive exploration, we aim to shed light on the intricate dynamics of how fast food choices can significantly influence your overall well-being. Beyond mere sustenance, food plays a pivotal role in shaping our physical health, mental well-being, and lifestyle choices.

Digestive System

Almost all fast food from American fast food franchises, including drinks and side dishes, contains carbohydrates and almost no fiber. When the digestive system breaks it down, the carbohydrates enter the bloodstream as glucose (sugar). As a result, a person's blood sugar levels change.

The pancreas responds to the release of glucose by releasing insulin. This hormone transports sugar to cells that need it for energy. As the body uses or stores sugar, blood sugar levels return to normal.

Eating large amounts of carbohydrates frequently can lead to repeated spikes in blood sugar. Over time, these insulin spikes can disrupt your body's normal insulin response. This increases the risk of insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and weight gain.

Danger to the reproductive system

A 2016 scientific paper shows that processed foods from stores and fast food restaurants contain phthalates, and these substances can disrupt hormones in the human body. Consuming foods high in this chemical can lead to reproductive problems, including birth defects.

Impact on bones and tissues

Carbohydrates and sugar in processed foods increase acidity levels in the mouth. And this affects tooth enamel and can destroy it. The weight gain that fast food can cause leads to poor bone density and poor quality muscle tissue.

High salt content

The combination of fat, sugar and a lot of salt makes fast food taste better. However, a diet high in salt and sodium can cause a person to feel puffy and bloated after eating fast food.

For people with high blood pressure, a diet high in sodium is dangerous. Sodium can increase blood pressure and put stress on the heart and cardiovascular system. A 2017 study shows that 90 percent of people don't think about the amount of sodium they consume when eating at fast food restaurants.
